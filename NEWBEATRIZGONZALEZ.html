For more than five decades, Beatriz González has excelled as a historian, art critic, and artist. In 1965, two versions of "Los suicides del Sisga" (The Sisga Suicidals), inspired by a photograph published in a local newspaper, made her worthy of the special painting prize of the XVII Salón de Artistas Nacionales and marked the beginning of a trajectory whose theme has been closely related to the social and political reality of the country. Beatriz González is, first of all, a painter, but she does not limit herself to working on canvas. In fact, she uses the most diverse surfaces and textures, including household objects such as towels and furniture, which she paints with oil, enamel, or acrylic. She has also made a large number of prints.
Beatriz González.Decoración de Interiores (Interior decoration), 1981. Color silkscreen on fabric. 106,2 x 118 inches. Editor: Taller de Victor Alfandari. Courtesy: Museo La Tertulia

The exhibition "Beatriz González: Graphic Notes" presents close to one hundred works made with mechanical or digital techniques such as engraving, lithography, screen printing, and digital printing. Among the pieces exhibited there are a series of engravings that make reference to the history of Western art, "Retrato de Rembrandt" (Rembrandt's Portrait, 1966); appropriations of photographs published by sensationalist newspapers, “Asesinada mujer en hospedaje…" (Woman Murdered in a Lodge..., 1969); advertisement, "Gimnasio Bacatá” (Bacatá Gym, 1968), and historical figures, "Apuntes para la historia extensa no. 1. Boceto (Notes for Extensive History no. 1. Sketch, 1968). Similarly, several serigraphs and holographs such as "Happy Birthday" (1970), “Lesa majestad” (Aggrieved Majesty, 1974) and "Jacqueline Onassis" (1975), show how González's work has been characterized by the use of flat colors and harmonic compositions since the beginning.

A fundamental aspect throughout her career is the political issue. In “Decoración de interiores” (Interior Decoration, 1981), a silkscreen on canvas in which President Julio César Turbay appears at a reception, as well as in "Zócalo de la Comedia" (Comedy’s Plinth, 1983) and "Zócalo de la tragedia" (Tragedy’s Plinth, 1983), the artist criticizes the corrupt politician and denounces the contrasts that exist in a country that experiences a civil war. In "Boceto. Fin de la fiesta/Los Papagayos” (Sketch. End of the Party/Los Papagayos, 1986), another president, Belisario Betancur, appears accompanied by the military, in explicit criticism of the resurgence of armed actions, in particular the siege of the Palace of Justice, which occurred in 1985.

As the armed conflict intensified, the artist became even more sensitive and began to represent herself and show women covering their faces, crying, in the face of the tragedy that plagued the country. The victims of the conflict are represented in works such as “Túmulo funerario para soldados bachilleres” (Burial Mound for High School Soldiers, 1986) and "Papel de colgadura. Predicadores” (Wallpaper. Preachers, 2002). The problem of displacement, both nationally and internationally, is illustrated in works such as "Zulia Zulia Zulia. Cenefa" (Zulia Zulia Zulia. Border, 2015) and "Papel de Colgadura. Desplazados” (Wallpaper. Displaced, 2017).

Finally, the exhibition includes her most recent serigraph, " Anteproyecto para un mueble inmueble” (Preliminary Project for a Real Estate Piece of Furniture, 2019), which represents one of the preparatory drawings for "Auras Anónimas" (Anonymous Auras) (2007-2009). In this project, the artist covered 8,957 cavities of the neoclassical buildings of Bogota's Central Cemetery with drawings representing the "cargueros," men carrying the bodies of the victims of violence in Colombia.
